Contour Integration (for Dummies)

First, we should motivate contour integration. Usually, people "motivate" a technique by trivialising some contrived example. I would prefer to motivate contour integration by explaining why it should help us:

Contour should be more powerful than basic integration techniues, since we can apply e.g the 'residue theorem' (a consequence of Stokes') to evaluate easily along closed contours. We essentially unlock more of the power of Stokes' theorem.

Residues

From our introduction to Stokes' theorem, we know that the integral around some closed contour is $0$, assuming all the things in the Stokes' expression exist. Here is a refresher: \[\int_\Omega d \omega = \int_{\partial \Omega } \omega ,\] but the LHS might not exist.

For example, we might assume the integral of $f(z)$ around the above contour to be $0$, but if $f$ has a pole at $i$ then this isn't the case.

Instead, we would have to wrap our contour around the singularity as closely as possible, and see what results. I will guide you through the process:

  • We would like to find the integral $\int_\gamma \frac{f(z)}{z-z_0}$, i.e there is a singularity at $z_0$.
  • We need to make some kind of substitution $z=g(\theta)$, which turns our integral into one around the circle $|z-z_0|=r$.
