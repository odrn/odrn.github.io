Probability Paradox

Problem:

There are two distinct positive integers $X,Y$, each written on separate pieces of paper. $X,Y$ were not necessarily picked with any distribution; in fact you know nothing about their distribution at all. You may look at $X$, and you are asked to guess if $Y$ is bigger or smaller than $X$.

You have access to a random number generator which generates uniformly random numbers on $(0,1)$.

Find a strategy which is correct with probability greater than $0.5$, regardless of the distribution of $(X,Y)$.


Solution:

Notice that we can actually generate random reals on $(1,\infty)$ by taking the reciprocal of a random on $(0,1)$. We will generate a real $r \in (1, \infty)$. Our strategy will be to guess e.g $X>Y$ if $X>r$. Now, there are $4$ ways we can be correct:

  • Pick $X$, $(X>Y)$, $X>r$
  • Pick $X$, $(X< Y)$, $X < r$
  • Pick $Y$, $(X>Y)$, $Y< r $
  • Pick $Y$, $(X< Y)$, $Y >r$

    • This gives us an expression for the probability we are correct, as follows: \[ P=0.5(P(X>Y,X>r) + P(X< Y , X > r) + P(Y > X,Y>r) + P(Y< X, Y< r)), \]where the $0.5$ is because we have an equal chance of seeing $X,Y$.

    We can combine some probabiliites: \[P(X>Y, X>r) + P(X>Y, Y< r) = P(X>Y)+P(X>Y, Y < r < X).\]Therefore our expression becomes \[P=0.5(P(X>Y) + P(X>Y, Y< r < X) + P(X < Y) + P(X < Y, Y < r < X)),\] which clearly simplifies to \[P=0.5(1+P(Y < r < X))>0.5,\] so we are done.

    What Just Happened?

    It seems like we can guess something about a random number, given another totally random number. This is of course unhelpful in the real world, since we can't do this kind of random number generation. Well, we can only outperform random guessing by the amount $0.5P(Y < r < X)$, which depends on the distribution that the "setter" is using to pick $X,Y$.

    In summary, it is indeed true that our "probability of being correct" is strictly greater than one half, but the performance depends on the distribution being used.
