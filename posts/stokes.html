Stokes' Theorem

Here I discuss (on a high/intuitive level) the general Stokes' theorem, with a view towards contour integration. This is intended as an exposition rather than any kind of detailed analysis. The reader should know some very basic topology, analysis, and linear algebra.

The Players

#

Let's introduce manifolds and differential forms.

Manifolds

A manifold is a locally flat (topological) space. This means "if I zoom in far enough, the space looks Euclidean". For example: consider a differentiable curve in the plane, like $y=x^2$. One can endow this set of points with a topology, and if we zoom in far enough at one point then it will look like the "tangent space" $y=2x$. Any "smooth-looking" curve or surface you can think of is probably a manifold. Manifolds are useful because they are a very general object and we can use calculus on them.

When we do contour integration, we will be integrating over a curve in the manifold $\mathbb{C}$.

Differential Forms

A differential form is a kind of expression. It does not describe any specific set of points. Rather, forms generalise the idea of $\mathrm{d}x$ being some change in the plane. A differential form on $\mathbb{R^2}$ might be any of the following:

  • $f(x)dx$
  • $3dx + 2dy$
  • $dxdy$.

    • Rather than themselves being any specific change, they can be thought to assign a change (vector) to each point on the manifold. For example, $dx$ would assign the same vector to each point, while $f(x) dx$ would probably assign different vectors at each point on the manifold.

    Let's look in some more detail: $dxdy$ is confusing at first glance - how can we multiply these functions? Is this a composition of functions? We will look at this type of multiplication in the exterior product post. For now, we will just look at $1$-forms like $dx$:

    If $\omega$ is a differential $1$-form on a manifold $M$, then for each $p \in M$, $\omega : T_p(M) \to \mathbb{R}$, where I use $\mathbb{R}$ instead of a general field $K$ for simplicity. Therefore $\omega$ is a set of linear functionals, and it knows when to apply each one.

    Forms and Calculus

    Differential forms can be used to generalise the derivative: we might write \[df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}dy, \] where $df$ is called the "total" derivative of $f(x,y)$.

    The fundamental theorem of calculus will intuitively generalise to $\int_{\gamma} df = f(b)-f(a)$, where $a,b$ are the endpoints of the curve $\gamma$.

    What does this mean for $\int_\gamma df$ if $\gamma$ is closed?

    Above, I used the integral sign to denote the integral in the following way: if $\omega$ is a differential form, then \[\int_\gamma \omega \approx \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} \omega_{x_i}(\Delta x_i), \] where $\omega_{x_i}$ is the "change" which $\omega$ associates with the point $x_i$. In linear algebra terms, $\omega_{x_i}$ is a linear form/functional. Of course, the integral and the sum become equal as $\sup |\Delta x_i| \to 0$.

    The Theorem

    Stokes' theorem involves a differential form $\omega$ and a manifold $\Omega$. It states that \[\int_{\partial \Omega} \omega = \int_\Omega d \omega.\] We might as well call this the fundamental theorem of calculus - it is in perfect analogy to the real case we are all used to.

    As you (hopefully) noted earlier, the integral around a closed loop should be $0$. Importantly, we assumed that everything in the expression for Stokes' theorem existed - for example, $d \omega$ might not have been integrable on the whole of $\Omega$, putting a spanner in the works. We will remedy this in the contour integration post.
