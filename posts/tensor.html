Tensor Product

Motivation

Our goal is to find the most natural way of "multiplying" differential forms - that is, some way of combining forms which obeys intuitive properties like $dx \times dy = -dy \times dx$ (reversing orientation should reverse sign), or $dx \times (dy + dz)=dx \times dy + dx \times dz$. These properties encode our geometric intuition for how integrating a combination of forms should work.

The tensor product is the most general kind of vector product we can define, and by imposing an extra condition afterwards we will obtain the "exterior product", of huge importance in calculus.

How It Works

Bilinear Mappings Become Linear Mappings

Bilinear mappings take in two vectors and spit out a number (an element of the field $K$). They are ways of "multiplying" two vectors (like the inner product). They are called bilinear since they are linear in each argument (think of how you could split out $(ax+by) \cdot (cu+dv)$).

The tensor product is the vector space $V \otimes W$ such that for any bilinear mapping $B:V \times W \to K$, there is a unique linear mapping $\phi_B: V \otimes W \to K$.

That is, the tensor product lets us use linear algebra on bilinear mappings.

The Universal Property

The universal property of the tensor product just says that "for any bilinear mapping $B:V \times W \to K$, there is a unique linear mapping $\phi_B: V \otimes W \to K$". I used this as the definition of the tensor product since it happens to work, as demonstrated here, but it is most important to understand the "universal property" that identifies bilinear mappings with linear ones. Specifically (read the next part carefully), if there is a bilinear mapping $B: V \times W \to K$, then the element $v \otimes w \in V \otimes W$ is the element such that the $\phi_B : V \otimes W \to K$ which corresponds to $B$ has $\phi(v \otimes w) = B(v,w)$. (The universal property basically says that a unique linear $\phi_B$ exists for each $B$, and the construction of the tensor product gives us the nice relationship above.)
